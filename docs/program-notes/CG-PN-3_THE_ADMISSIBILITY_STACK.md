# Program Note #3 — The Admissibility Stack

*Permission, not inertia.*

This note is not a paper. It introduces no evaluators, thresholds, methods, or operational criteria. It maps the structural conditions under which claims of continuation remain well-formed.

**Track:** Admissibility Studies (AS)
**Program:** The Constraint Program
**Posture:** Stage-0 / CRL-0 — observer-only, non-authoritative

---

## Opening axiom

Systems do not continue by default.

They continue by permission.

Before describing dynamics, before evaluating outcomes, and before invoking optimization or control, a prior question must be addressed:

What makes a continuation claim structurally admissible?

---

## Scope of this note

This Program Note introduces the Admissibility Stack — a minimal structural map describing the conditions under which continuation claims remain coherent.

The stack does not evaluate systems. It disciplines the conditions under which structural claims remain coherent.

---

## 1. Primitive distinctions

The stack begins by fixing three primitives that must not be collapsed:

**Identity** — A claim about admissible continuation of a system.

**Memory** — Persistence of encoded state.

**Record** — A representation that crosses a boundary in receiptable form.

These primitives are structurally distinct.

Identity requires continuity. Memory requires encoded persistence. Record requires boundary coupling.

A system may retain traces without possessing memory, emit records without preserving identity, or preserve identity while exchanging its constituents.

None of the three are equivalent.

---

## 2. Relations governing the primitives

Each primitive is governed by a distinct relation.

**Continuity** governs identity. Identity is not sameness of material components but the admissible extension of a trajectory.

**Encoded persistence** governs memory. Memory concerns the stability of internal state under coupling.

**Boundary coupling** governs record. Records arise when internal structure becomes externally receiptable.

These relations ensure that identity, memory, and record remain structurally distinguishable.

---

## 3. Domain invariants

Persistent structure exists within two domain constraints.

**Universal Coupling Constraint** — No subsystem remains fully isolated from the constraint relations governing its domain.

**Resolution-Depth Constraint** — Structure can persist only at depths compatible with the descriptive resolution available to sustain it.

Together these invariants define the limits within which persistence can remain structurally coherent.

---

## 4. Admissible claim classes

Within those limits, the stack disciplines the claims that may be asserted.

**Continuation** — Continuation is primitive and binary: either continuation is permitted or it is not.

**Unitarity** — If continuation is permitted, unitarity asks whether the admissible extension is unique.

**Ontic vs epistemic order** — The order of receipts does not determine ontic precedence. What can be observed does not exhaust what may exist.

These distinctions ensure that claims about persistence remain structurally disciplined.

---

## 5. Resolution effects

Observation introduces its own structural effects.

**Projection / coarse-graining** — Descriptions often compress underlying structure.

**Epistemic non-uniqueness** — Multiple admissible continuations may appear at a given observational resolution even when underlying evolution remains unique.

**Apparent irreversibility** — Projection may produce asymmetries that do not originate in the underlying structure.

These effects belong to observation, not ontology.

---

## 6. Bounded subsystems

Within regimes of persistence, subsystems may stabilize.

**Agency** — A bounded subsystem with stable internal encoding may restrict which macro continuations remain admissible at its operating resolution.

Agency here describes structural restriction, not control or optimization.

**Macro freedom** — Freedom at the macro scale appears when multiple continuations remain admissible under projection and are resolved through internal state.

Freedom does not arise from absence of constraint. It arises from multiple admissible trajectories within constraint.

---

## 7. The cost of remaining defined

Persistence is not free.

Structures that remain defined must maintain internal organization while coupled to their environment.

Two costs appear:

**Ledger cost** — The structural overhead required to maintain internal encoding.

**Boundary rent** — The dissipation required to keep internal structure stable under coupling.

**Constraint affordability** — A system remains defined at a given resolution only insofar as it can sustain the costs required to maintain its boundary and internal state.

Persistence therefore selects for affordable boundaries.

---

## Closing

The Admissibility Stack does not evaluate systems, prescribe dynamics, or propose methods.

It simply clarifies the structural conditions under which claims of persistence remain well-formed.

Before describing dynamics, before assigning causes, before invoking optimization or control, a prior question must be addressed:

What makes continuation admissible at all?

Admissibility Studies begins there.

Because "continue" is not inertia. It is a governed act.
